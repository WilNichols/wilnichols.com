I began writing this on Sunday March 1st, on one of a few flights my company took to our week-long working retreat in Destin, FL. I got about halfway through, we made our connecting flight, and then our CEO sat down next to me. I felt like continuing the neither my doom nor gloom visibly, and we spent the time discussing an impressive video calling platform he’s building in his tinkering hours. 

I then spent the next week working long but engaging hours on and around the beach, having a great time with my colleagues, taking plenty of breaks to play tennis, and trying not to think *too much* about the state of the world. 

Of course, I failed at that, and this unrequited itch has grown in the back of my head until yesterday, my first day back from the hackathon and working typical hours, I realized I was avoidantly distracting myself from deep work *or* staring at the wall for so long that the texture began to swim.

Anyway, I’d written:

> We rolled out of bed shortly before sunrise Saturday morning, and made our way to Wells Branch where we volunteer at the [St. Andrew's Food Pantry](https://staopen.org). With no time to read the news, it’s in catching up with the many volunteers that I learn that much earlier that morning, we[^1] bombed Iran. Of the many targets, over 50 were killed in a strike on a girls’ elementary school in Minab.[^2] Later that day, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was announced assassinated.[^3]
> 
> Likely being the first awake, I shared news in my friends’ group chat, and as they woke and engaged, my message was followed up with the 7:21am announcement of my friend’s nephew’s joyous birth.
> 
> U.S. CENTCOM issued a statement this morning that three U.S. service members were killed in action, in “Operation Epic Fury.” These detestable, murderous motherfuckers named their unsanctioned war after a nine-year-old’s raid on a rival Minecraft server. Or Elon chimed in.
> 
> I was born in ’94, in one of the increasingly rare windows that the United States wasn’t actively murdering people in the Middle East. I remember 9/11 and the following anti-war movement, and its evolution in Obama ’08’s *Change.*
> 
> [The Savage Wars of Peace](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/18244.The_Savage_Wars_Of_Peace)[^2] left my high school class with an understanding for our country’s predilection for violent regime change. [The Shock Doctrine](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/18244.The_Savage_Wars_Of_Peace) primed a smaller group of us for, among other things, the Trump administration’s love for flooding the field. What’s old is new, except us. We’re just older.
> 
> Despite this understanding of the violent terror of American Hegemony, I am still shocked by the my country’s and seemingly the world’s acceptance that the US President can unilaterally capture and assassinate foreign dignitaries. Regardless of one’s position on the Maduro administration or Khamenei’s regime, American aggression must be curtailed and swiftly punished.
> 
> I remember the early antiwar movement, and Twitter’s later role in sharing the Arab Spring with the world. I remember it as a distribution of live reporting and information sharing that enabled a more informed population. I mourn that now, despite mobile tech’s social proliferation and the then-unimagined extent of our social networks, our tech and tools distract and surveil, where they could have informed, organized, and empowered. Were we — and I’m sure we do — to have a 2026 Flint Water Crisis, it would be drowned on X and soft-banned on TikTok. We have failed.
> 
> In fact, it [came to light](https://www.404media.co/how-ice-and-cbp-use-free-walkie-talkie-app-zello-to-power-their-operations/) that ICE uses Zello’s Friends & Family service. We, too, have failed.

What a place to end as Alex sat down in my neighboring aisle seat. Since then, I’ve heard that some employees have found the article, but discussion’s limited. Individuals are scared in this climate, and many who would otherwise make noise recognize that ours is a company of immigrants and other vulnerable people. Drawing attention to ourselves is a risky and shortsighted move, while few are comfortable in doing nothing.

So we spent the week near Destin, in private rentals in the Sandestin resort. Between one employee having been profiled by private security for trying to open his house,  others for _shopping_, and finally a resident approaching two employees with a knife in his hand as he tremulously asked what they were doing, Florida maintains its glowing reputation as one of the least hospitable states in our failing Union. 

We spent the week building cool and exciting enhancements to our product, sales pipeline, and marketing processes. With eyes blinded to the outside world we suspended our disbelief and (some of) our rage at the now-daily atrocity and told ourselves that Zello saves lives by being used in natural disasters, search and rescue, and EMS. Hell, Zello was used to [catch a double murder suspect](https://zmones-15min-lt.translate.goog/naujiena/raskime-zudika-kas-ta-zello-programele-kuria-naudojantis-sugautas-itariamasis-benas-mikutavicius-xvVZGv305g4?_x_tr_sl=auto&_x_tr_tl=en&_x_tr_hl=en&_x_tr_pto=wapp&_x_tr_hist=true) in Lithuania this last winter. 

How could the app that was used by [Turkish protestors in 2013](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2013/jun/04/turkish-protestors-encryption-software-evade-censors) and [Venezuelan protestors in 2014](https://www.businessinsider.com/zello-used-by-venezuelan-protestors-2014-2) now be on the wrong side of a conflict? Like much of American tech, we can oppose governments in the Global South, or even [our founding employees' mother country](https://mashable.com/article/russia-limits-zello-walkie-talkie-app-ukraine), but opposing the modern American gestapo is dicier. 

So we’re back from our trip, and I’m sitting at my desk yesterday, trying to work but pouring my mind and soul disassociatively into the wall. Were the wallpaper yellow it’d be poetic, but the new-build-landlord-special has to do. 

Friday, Al Jazeera reported that [a fourth school](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/6/elementary-school-in-tehran-hit-irans-foreign-ministry-says) was struck by American missiles. UNICEF declared that nearly [200 children have been murdered](https://www.news18.com/world/unicef-reports-almost-200-children-killed-in-attacks-as-hostilities-escalate-in-west-asia-conflict-ws-l-9945398.html) in the first week of this genocide. Yesterday, I’m staring through the wall, imagining the unlivable blanket of heavy-metal-laden smoke and black acid rain pouring over Tehran following joint Israeli-American airstrikes on [four oil facilities](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cqxd1nv3re2o). 

I’m unsure how to live responsibly and sanely in these times, and while Darien and I progress towards leaving the country — our visas are secured and tickets are scheduled for April 7 — I know that the my busyness prevents spiraling on the evils surrounding us. They’re going nowhere, and I hope that from outside the U.S. we can do more than we can here.
